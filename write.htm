Can Ted pat the red cat?

Here are some sentences with simple words and commonly briefed words. Here is a diagram of the layout. Concentrate on first learning the keys that aren't greyed out. The vowels in the bottom row are pressed with the thumbs. The consonants in the left side are written with the left hand and are called initial consonants. The consonants in the right side are written with the right hand and called final consonants. To write a simple one syllable word, use the vowel keys to write the vowel in the middle of the word, the initial consonants to write consonants before it, and the final consonants to write consonants after it. And press all of the keys in the word at the same time. However, some words are usually written in a simplified way because they are very common, so look at the table and try to learn the words in it. You might notice there are two initial S keys. Pressing one, the other or both has the same effect, so just use whichever you find easiest.

The asterisk in the middle is a wildcard for things like disambiguating homophones. But in this exercise you'll have to use it for something else: Plover's default dictionary uses PUR as a brief for "purchaser" because that word is much more common than "purr", so you'll have to use the asterisk key to write "purr". Also, if you make a mistake, you can use the asterisk as a backspace of sorts that undoes the last stroke.

The little cat

Longer words might have to be written with more than one stroke. Sometimes you split them into syllables and write one stroke per syllable. Other times you leave out some sounds, such as unstressed vowels.

Here's a table with the same keys you used in the previous lesson plus O, -F, -L and -Z. Let's look at the consonants first: -F can represent an F sound or sometimes a V sound or an S sound. L, for now, just represents an L sound. -Z is like a second -S sound for those cases where you want to have an -S sound after another -S sound. Or after the -tion suffix which we won't care about in this lesson.

S T P H * F P L T D
S K W R * R B G S Z
    A O   E U

So now that you know how to write final F and L, you're probably wondering how to write those sounds at the start of syllables: the initial F is made by pressing T and P together and the initial L is made by pressing H and R together.

And now for the vowels: O is the sound in words like stop and hot. If you're not sure when to use O and when to use U, it's usually the spelling that decides, unless the word is spelt with both O and U, in which case U usually wins as long as it doesn't conflict with some other word (so cough will be written with O so as not to conflict with cuff.

Another vowel you might want to write is I. The short vowel I is written by pressing E and U at the same time. And English has two diff things that contain that sound: AI and OI. The former, as in "stain" and "sane" is written AEU. The latter, found in the word "coil" is written OEU.

Kåre så på skak

Her er nogle vokaler, foreløbig kan I godt se bort fra længde og stød:
  • A, sådan som det udtales i "skak"
  • Æ, som i "kæk"
  • AÆ, et almindelight a, som i "skat"
  • Å, som en "å"
  • O, som i "klokke".
  • Foreløbig kan i lade være med at skrive e'erne der er efter konsonanter i slutningen af ord. Det kan man når dét, man skriver, ikke kommer til, at blive forvekslet med et andet af, at man springer e'et over.
Dem skriver vi med tommelfingrene. Konsonanter skriver vi med de andre fingre, og så bruger vi venstre hånd til dem, der skal stå før vokalerne og højre hånd til dem, der skal stå efter dem. Her kan I se hvor tasterne er på stenografitastaturet. Bare se bort fra de grå, dem skal vi nok se på senere. 
-N T P H * F P L T D
 S K V R * R E K S D
     A O   Æ Å
Kåre så på skak og Rikke så på kar. Kåre kan så og Rikke er kæk. Er Saras kat kær? Kåre er kok. Rikkes sok er på Rikkes skab. Kåre kan skære kager. Er Rikke skrap? Råb og reb. Sara er rap.

Kåre så på skak

Her er nogle flere vokaler:
  • ÆÅ, en almindelig E-lyd, som i'et i "blik"
  • AOÆ, en I-lyd som i "klit"
  • AU, en almindelight O-lyd, som i "flod"
  • E, en E-lyd i slutningen af it ord. Men nogle gange kan man godt underforstå dén lyd.
Og så får vi også nogle konsonanter:
  • T og -T
  • F, som i starten af stavelser skrives som TP. Når S skal stå efter vokaler men før andre konsonanter i samme stavelse kan det skrives som -F.
  • L, som i starten af stavelser skrives som HR
